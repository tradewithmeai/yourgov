# UK Parliament — Plain-English Glossary

Reference for an LLM explainer that defines parliamentary terms a citizen meets in **YourGov** (yourgov.solvx.uk), which shows how MPs voted on House of Commons divisions, by constituency, from the public record.

## How to use this glossary (rules the explainer inherits)

- **Factual only.** Define the term and, where useful, relate it to what YourGov shows (recorded Commons divisions: who voted Aye, who voted No, tellers, and the seat each MP holds).
- **Never infer intent.** A recorded vote shows *what* an MP did, never *why*. Do not state or imply motivation, guilt, corruption, hypocrisy, or character.
- **Absence is not evidence.** A missing vote or record is not proof of wrongdoing or disinterest. Some MPs are *legitimately voteless*: the Speaker, Deputy Speakers (while in the Chair), abstentionist-party MPs (Sinn Féin), and newly elected MPs with no division yet.
- **No rankings or verdicts.** No "best/worst MP". No "broke a promise" language unless backed by source-linked pledge evidence.
- **State limits.** Distinguish what the public record *shows* from what it does *not* prove. Records can lag current events or be incomplete.
- **No recommendations.** Do not tell anyone how to vote or what to think.

## Voting in the Commons

**Division** — The formal procedure by which the House of Commons records a vote: members physically walk through one of two lobbies to be counted, so each MP's choice is recorded by name. Every row YourGov shows about a vote comes from a division.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_of_the_assembly

**Aye / No (and the division lobby)** — "Aye" is a vote in favour of the question; "No" is a vote against. MPs register these by walking through the **Aye lobby** or the **No lobby** (the two corridors beside the Commons chamber) where their names are recorded.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_of_the_assembly

**Teller** — One of (usually) four MPs — two per side — appointed to physically count the members passing through each lobby and report the totals to the Speaker. Tellers are listed separately and are not themselves counted in the Aye/No totals they report.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teller_(elections)

**Abstention** — Choosing not to cast a vote either way. An MP may abstain by staying out of both lobbies; an abstention is not recorded as Aye or No, so it can look like a missing vote even though no decision-for-or-against was made.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstention

**Abstentionism / abstentionist parties (Sinn Féin)** — The deliberate practice of winning a seat but refusing to take it up or vote. Sinn Féin MPs do not sit or vote in the Commons as a matter of long-standing policy, so zero recorded votes for them is expected, not a data gap.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstentionism

## People and roles in the Chamber

**The Speaker** — The MP elected to chair Commons proceedings impartially and keep order. By convention the Speaker does not vote, except to break a tie (a casting vote), so the Speaker normally appears in the record with no votes — this is expected.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speaker_of_the_House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)

**Deputy Speaker (Chairman of Ways and Means)** — One of the Speaker's deputies who chairs the House when the Speaker is absent. Like the Speaker, a Deputy Speaker does not vote while in the Chair, so their recorded votes are reduced or absent by design.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_Ways_and_Means

## Party discipline

**Party whip** — Both the party officials who manage voting discipline and the formal instruction they issue telling MPs how the party wants them to vote on a given division. "Having the whip" means sitting as a member of that party's parliamentary group.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whip_(politics)

**Three-line whip** — The strongest form of whip instruction (the line under the request is emphasised three times), signalling that attendance and a vote along party lines are treated as essential. Defying it is the most serious breach of party discipline.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whip_(politics)

**Rebellion / voting against the party whip** — When an MP votes contrary to their party's instruction. The record shows that an MP voted differently from their party on a division; it does not, on its own, explain the reason.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Party_discipline

## How a law is made

**Bill** — A proposed law as it passes through Parliament. A bill is only a proposal and has no legal force until it completes its stages and receives Royal Assent.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_(law)

**Act (Act of Parliament)** — A bill that has completed all parliamentary stages and received Royal Assent, becoming law. "Bill" = proposal; "Act" = enacted law.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Act_of_parliament

**First / second / third reading** — The three main stages a bill passes through in each House: the **first reading** formally introduces it (no debate), the **second reading** is the main debate and vote on its principle, and the **third reading** is the final review-and-vote on the (possibly amended) bill before it moves on. Many YourGov divisions are second- or third-reading votes.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_(legislature)

**Royal Assent** — The Monarch's formal approval that turns a bill into an Act. It is a constitutional formality, not a political decision, and is the point at which a bill becomes law.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_assent

**Motion** — A formal proposal put to the House for a decision. MPs debate and then vote on the motion (the "question"); a division is the count of that vote.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_(parliamentary_procedure)

**Amendment** — A proposed change to the wording of a bill or motion — adding, removing, or altering text. Amendments are themselves voted on, so a single bill can generate several distinct divisions.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amendment

**Private Member's Bill** — A bill introduced by an MP (or peer) who is not a government minister, rather than by the Government. Such bills get limited time and often fail, but some become law.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_member%27s_bill

**Statutory instrument / delegated legislation** — Secondary law made by ministers using powers granted by an Act, allowing detailed rules without a full new bill. Parliament scrutinises these instruments, sometimes by a division to approve or annul them.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statutory_instrument_(UK)

## Parliament, parties, and seats

**Government vs Opposition** — The Government is formed by the party (or coalition) that commands a Commons majority and provides ministers; the **Opposition** is made up of the parties that are not in government, the largest of which forms the Official Opposition. The Government usually proposes the business that divisions vote on.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom · https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_opposition

**The Commons vs the Lords** — Parliament's two Houses. The **House of Commons** is the elected chamber of MPs (one per constituency) and is where the divisions YourGov tracks take place; the **House of Lords** is the unelected revising chamber that scrutinises and amends legislation.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom · https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Lords

**Constituency / seat** — A geographic area that elects one MP to the Commons. The UK has 650 constituencies (= 650 seats); YourGov holds one row per seat, and a seat with no current MP is shown explicitly as a "Vacant seat", never omitted.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constituencies_of_the_Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom · https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_district

**By-election** — An election held to fill a single seat that has fallen vacant between general elections (for example after an MP resigns or dies). A by-election explains why a constituency may briefly be vacant or have a newly elected MP with no voting history yet.
More: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/By-election
